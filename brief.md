# Mark Hachem: Website Review, Gallery Profile, and Due-Diligence Assessment

## Website review

Mark Hachem’s website presents a credible contemporary art gallery with an established exhibition program, but the site itself feels uneven in execution. Its main structure is clear and easy to navigate, with sections for artists, exhibitions, collections, fairs, publications, and contact information. At a glance, it communicates the gallery’s commercial and curatorial identity effectively.

The main weakness is inconsistency. Some sections appear current and informative, especially the exhibitions, fairs, and contact pages, while other areas feel dated or incomplete. The homepage includes older-looking material and generic filler-style text, which creates a mismatch with the stronger, more current exhibition archive. This gives the impression of a website that has been updated in parts rather than comprehensively maintained.

The artist and exhibition sections are more convincing than the homepage. The site shows a broad international roster and an active archive of exhibitions in Paris and Beirut, which supports the impression of a functioning gallery with ongoing programming. Overall, the website succeeds in establishing legitimacy, but it does not fully reflect the apparent stature of the gallery. It feels functional rather than polished.

## Gallery profile

Mark Hachem appears to be an established international gallery with roots in Paris and an expanded presence in New York and Beirut. External profiles identify the gallery as having been founded in Paris in 1996, with later expansion into New York in 2007 and Beirut in 2010. The gallery’s own contact information aligns with this multi-city identity.

Its program appears to span both modern and contemporary art, with particular strength in Arab modernism and kinetic art. That positioning is consistent with both third-party descriptions and the gallery’s own exhibition material. The roster mixes Middle Eastern, European, and Latin American artists, suggesting a cross-regional model rather than a narrowly local one.

The gallery also appears active in art fairs and commercial platforms. Recent fair listings and external marketplace references indicate continued participation in international events, reinforcing the view that the gallery remains active and professionally networked.

## Due-diligence assessment

The gallery appears legitimate and active. Third-party recognition from established art-world platforms, along with recent exhibition and fair activity, are strong indicators that Mark Hachem is a real and functioning gallery business.

The main caution point is not legitimacy but presentation quality. The website contains signs of uneven upkeep, including stale-looking homepage content and inconsistencies between pages. That suggests the digital presence is not the gallery’s strongest asset and should not be treated as the sole source of operational detail.

From a due-diligence standpoint, the gallery looks credible enough for further engagement, but important details should still be verified directly before making decisions related to acquisition, consignment, partnership, or press outreach. The strongest signals are the gallery’s external validation, long operating history, and recent programming. The weakest signal is the website’s inconsistent maintenance.

## Overall conclusion

Mark Hachem comes across as a legitimate, internationally active gallery whose real-world profile is stronger than its website. The site provides enough evidence of recent exhibitions, fair participation, and artist representation to support credibility, but its uneven execution slightly weakens the gallery’s digital impression. In practical terms, the gallery appears worthy of attention, while the website should be read as a partial reflection of the business rather than a polished institutional presentation.
